The Head and the Body

Christ and the Ekklesia — organism, not organisation

"And He put all things in subjection under His feet, and gave Him as head over all things to the church, which is His body, the fullness of Him who fills all in all."

Ephesians 1:22–23

The ekklesia is not an organisation with Christ as its figurehead. It is a living organism with Christ as its literal, functional head. The head directs. The body responds. Every member is connected to the head — and to each other.

The contrast
Headship Pastor as functional head; Christ as figurehead
Headship Christ as actual, directing head; no human substitute
Ministry One man speaks — the rest listen
Ministry Every member functions according to their gift
Structure Audience and performer — spectators in pews
Structure Living organism — a body with interdependent parts
Growth From the programme — events, sermons, schedules
Growth From the head — "from whom the whole body grows"
Result Most members atrophy — unused, ungifted, passive
Result The whole body, "joined and held together," builds itself up in love
The issue

The modern church has essentially created a body with one giant mouth and hundreds of ears — while the hands, feet, and eyes atrophy from disuse. The eye cannot say to the hand, "I have no need of you." But the institutional model says exactly this — silently, structurally, every Sunday.

The question is not whether the church needs leadership. The question is whether any human being can stand where only Christ is meant to stand — as the functional, directing, life-giving head of His own body.

Christ as head
Headship Established
Ephesians 1:22–23
"And He put all things in subjection under His feet, and gave Him as head over all things to the church, which is His body, the fullness of Him who fills all in all."
The Father gave Christ as head — not to an institution, but to the ekklesia. And the ekklesia is not a building or a denomination. It is His body. The body exists to express the head. The "fullness of Him" — the church is meant to be Christ's full expression in the earth.

Growing Up into the Head
Ephesians 4:15–16
"But speaking the truth in love, we are to grow up in all aspects into Him who is the head, even Christ, from whom the whole body, being fitted and held together by what every joint supplies, according to the proper working of each individual part, causes the growth of the body for the building up of itself in love."
Growth comes from the head. Not from the programme, the sermon series, or the pastoral vision. From Christ. And it comes through "every joint" and "each individual part." This is not optional language — the body cannot function with most of its parts switched off. The growth is organic, mutual, and connected. Each member supplies something no other can.

Holding Fast to the Head
Colossians 2:19
"…and not holding fast to the head, from whom the entire body, being supplied and held together by the joints and ligaments, grows with a growth which is from God."
Paul's warning in Colossians is about people who lose connection with the head. Not with the pastor. Not with the denomination. With Christ. When the body is connected to the head, it grows "with a growth which is from God" — not manufactured growth, not programme-driven growth, but the organic, supernatural increase that only comes from the head directing the body.

The Preeminence
Colossians 1:18
"He is also head of the body, the church; and He is the beginning, the firstborn from the dead, so that He Himself will come to have first place in everything."
First place in everything. Not first place in theory while someone else leads in practice. Not first place in theology while the programme runs the week. He Himself — first place — in everything. The moment any human system or human leader functionally displaces Christ's direct headship, something essential to the ekklesia has been lost.

The body and its members
Many Members, One Body
1 Corinthians 12:12–14
"For even as the body is one and yet has many members, and all the members of the body, though they are many, are one body, so also is Christ. For by one Spirit we were all baptised into one body… For the body is not one member, but many."
Not one member, but many. The genius of the body is diversity within unity. Every member has been placed by the Spirit — not volunteered, not assigned by a committee, but baptised into their position by God Himself. A body with one functioning part is not a body. It is a cripple.

No Member Expendable
1 Corinthians 12:15–22
"If the foot says, 'Because I am not a hand, I am not a part of the body,' it is not for this reason any the less a part of the body… The eye cannot say to the hand, 'I have no need of you'; or again the head to the feet, 'I have no need of you.' On the contrary, it is much truer that the members of the body which seem to be weaker are necessary."
Paul is not using an illustration. He is making an argument. No member is expendable. The "weaker" members are necessary. But the institutional model structurally communicates that one gift — preaching — is essential, while all others are optional extras. The members who "seem to be weaker" are precisely the ones the system renders invisible.

God Has Placed the Members
1 Corinthians 12:18, 24–25
"But now God has placed the members, each one of them, in the body, just as He desired… but God has so composed the body, giving more abundant honour to that member which lacked, so that there may be no division in the body, but that the members may have the same care for one another."
God placed the members. Not the church board. Not the pastor. God composed the body so that there would be no division, no hierarchy of importance, no members treated as dispensable. When the structure of the church silences most members, it contradicts the very arrangement God designed.

The warning and the recovery
When Everyone Functions
1 Corinthians 14:26
"What is the outcome then, brethren? When you assemble, each one has a psalm, has a teaching, has a revelation, has a tongue, has an interpretation. Let all things be done for edification."
This is what a gathering looked like when the head was directing. Not one man behind a pulpit — but "each one" bringing something. A psalm. A teaching. A revelation. The operative word is "each." The modern church has a name for this kind of gathering: chaos. Paul had a different name: normal.

The Equippers, Not the Performers
Ephesians 4:11–12
"And He gave some as apostles, and some as prophets, and some as evangelists, and some as pastors and teachers, for the equipping of the saints for the work of service, to the building up of the body of Christ."
The fivefold gifts exist to equip the saints — not to replace them. The pastor was never meant to be the one who does the ministry while the congregation watches. He was meant to equip them so that they do the ministry. The goal is not a better preacher. The goal is a functioning body.

The Whole Body
Romans 12:4–5
"For just as we have many members in one body and all the members do not have the same function, so we, who are many, are one body in Christ, and individually members one of another."
Members one of another. Not members of an organisation. Not attenders of a service. Not supporters of a vision. Members of each other — organically connected, mutually dependent, individually functioning. This is the ekklesia. Not what we attend on Sunday, but what we are in Christ.

The recovery

The recovery of the ekklesia is not about better worship services, better preaching, or better programmes. It is the recovery of Christ's headship in practice — not just in doctrine. It is every member discovering their function, finding their place, and learning to respond to the head rather than to a human system.

The body does not need a better mouth. It needs its hands, its feet, its eyes, its heart — all alive, all functioning, all held together by the one who is the head.

The church is not what we attend.
It is what we are —
His body, His fullness,
His expression in the earth.
