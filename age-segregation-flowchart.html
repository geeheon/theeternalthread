The Segregated Church
vs the Ekklesia

The New Testament knows nothing of a church sorted by age — only a family growing together

"The older women likewise… that they admonish the young women… the older men… likewise exhort the young men to be sober-minded."

Titus 2:2–6

Paul's instruction to Titus presupposes a community where older and younger are in regular, organic proximity — not sorted into separate rooms, but woven into the same fabric of shared life. When we separate the generations, we don't just lose convenience. We lose the very structure by which the New Testament says formation happens.

THE FAMILY TABLE
Imagine a household where the children eat in one room, the teenagers in another, and the adults in the dining room — every week, for years. Everyone is fed. But formation doesn't happen. Children never hear the real conversations. They never see how the older ones handle conflict, gratitude, grief. Then at eighteen they're handed a seat at the adult table and have no idea how to be there.

The family table is where you become family — not just where you eat.

DIAGNOSTIC
Why do teenagers leave the church at eighteen?
Tap to expand
Because they were never in it. They were in the youth group — a parallel institution with its own culture, its own leaders, its own style. When the youth group ends, there's nothing to transition into, because they were never part of the thing they're being asked to join. They didn't leave the ekklesia. They were never given a seat at the table.
Why do children find "real" church boring?
Tap to expand
Because they've been trained on a substitute — high-energy, entertainment-calibrated, designed to hold their attention. The actual gathering, where adults worship, wrestle, repent, and share life, looks dull by comparison. But it only looks dull because they've never been part of it. A child who grows up watching adults encounter Christ doesn't find it boring. They find it real.
Why is there no Titus 2 in your community?
Tap to expand
Because older and younger are never in the same room at the same time. Older women can't train younger women if they meet in separate programmes. Fathers in the faith — those who have "known Him who is from the beginning" — have no access to the children and young men who need them most. The inter-generational chain that Paul took for granted has been structurally severed. Then we create mentoring programmes to try to recreate artificially what should happen organically.
Why does spiritual maturity seem so rare?
Tap to expand
Because maturity is caught, not just taught. You don't become a father in the faith by attending a class about fatherhood. You become one by being around fathers — watching how they respond, how they pray, how they handle suffering, how they see God's purpose in everything. When the generations are separated, the younger never see maturity modelled. They only hear about it secondhand, pre-digested, in a curriculum.
